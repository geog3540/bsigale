Hurricane Evacuation Accessibility

Hurricanes are one of the most devastating natural distasters which can occur, causing hundreds of lives lost and billions in damages. One of the most crtical parts of hurricane preparedness is save evacuation routes, as the best way to protect yourself from a hurrican is to simply not go near it. The focus of this project is to examine the relationship between hurricane evacuation routes and social vulnerability, as defined and reported in the CDC's Sovial Vulneratbility Index (SVI). The SVI identifies important demographic data, such as unemployment rates and access to a vehicle, to rank census block groups in relation to one another. This was designed in partnership with the ATSDR's Geospatial Research, Analysis & Services Program (GRASP) to help first responders identify where communities were hit hardest. By integrating additional data, such as projected flood depths and building damage, this response can be augmented to further identify areas which need help most immediately.

This particular dataset was performed on a clipping of the SVI from Hydroshare.org containing the SVI information for areas affected by Hurricane Harvey. Hurricane Harvey ocurred in August 2017 and caused billions in damage to the Texas, Louisiana, and Alambama coasts, as well as significant damage in the Bahamas. This analysis was performed on data from Texas, as Texas has been notorious in past hurricanes to have millions stranded in highway traffic jams during hurricane evacuations. While this is not a problem exclusive to Texas by any means, Texas is a great location for this analysis as Houston, a very economically and socially diverse big city, was right in the middle of Hurricane Harvey.
